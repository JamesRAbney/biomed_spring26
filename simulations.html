The textbook Introductory Biomedical Imaging explores principles and practices behind light microscopy, which is used to study cells and tissues, and medical imaging, which is used to study organs and the body. There are four primary medical imaging techniques: (1) ultrasound imaging, (2) x-ray imaging, (3) radionuclide imaging, and (4) magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). This website includes a set of interactive simulations exploring these techniques. The simulations for a given technique can be reached by clicking on the technique below.

Ultrasound

Ultrasound imaging uses high- frequency sound to create real-time images. The sound is bounced off tissues and organs internal body structures. The time it takes to receive a reflection is used to determine distances; the strength of the reflection is used to find and assess interfaces (e.g., between tissues and organs).

X-Ray Imaging

X-ray imaging uses x-rays to form images. The X-rays are transmitted through body structures and detected on the opposite side. Two- dimensional images can be generated, such as the familiar chest X-ray. Alternatively, three-dimensional views can be generated by taking multiple views at multiple angles and calculating structure using computed tomography (CT). CT scans can be used for both soft tissue and bones.

Radionuclide Imaging

Radionuclide imaging uses gamma rays created by radioactive atoms to create functional images of the body. The radioactive atoms can be targeted to different bodily structures, including tumors, and used to create functional maps of the body.
